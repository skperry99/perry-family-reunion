Our Story

The Perry family story begins with two families who journeyed from Europe to Michigan’s Upper Peninsula – and continues across generations, states, and even continents.

Roots in Cornwall & Finland

William Henry Perry Sr. was the son of Cornish immigrants who came to the U.P. seeking work and opportunity. Irene Marie (Hakala) Perry was the daughter of Finnish immigrants who also made their home in Michigan.

Together, William and Irene raised nine children between 1939 and 1952, most of whom, along with many grandchildren, spent their early years in Michigan.

From the U.P. to Everywhere

Over time, work, school, and family led the Perry descendants far beyond Michigan – including Missouri and overseas. Today our family includes children, grandchildren, great-grandchildren, and great-great-grandsons who live in different states and countries.

This reunion is a chance to reconnect, put faces to names, and introduce the newest branches of the family tree – including little ones and relatives some of us may be meeting for the very first time.

Family Tree Snapshot

This is a simple, high-level look at how the branches of the Perry family fit together. It’s not a full genealogy chart, but it helps everyone see where they connect in the family story.

Founders

  • William Henry Perry Sr.
  • Irene “Irja” (Hakala) Perry
  • Married in 1939 in Ishpeming, Michigan.
  • Made their home in Michigan’s Upper Peninsula.
  • Parents of nine children (1939–1952).

Their Children (9)

William and Irene’s nine children form the “second generation” in this reunion. Many of us know them as Mom, Dad, Aunt, or Uncle.

  • Nine children born between 1939 and 1952.
  • Raised mainly in Michigan’s Upper Peninsula.
  • Later spread to Michigan, Missouri, Florida, Illinois, and beyond.
  • Four of the nine have passed away and are remembered on the In Loving Memory page.

Generations Today

  • Grandchildren: 18 grandchildren (born roughly 1965–1985), many of whom grew up in Michigan.
  • Great-grandchildren: Now spread across several states and even overseas.
  • Great-great-grandsons: At least two little ones in the newest generation.
  • Spouses, partners, and extended family members are all part of this gathering too.

The 2026 reunion brings together four generations – from those who remember the U.P. days to the great-great-grandsons meeting everyone for the first time.
